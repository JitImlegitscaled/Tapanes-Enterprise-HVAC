7 Energy-Efficiency Tips to Lower Your AC Bill This Summer

May 2026 6 min read Tapanes Enterprise LLC

Tampa Bay homeowners run their air conditioners an average of 8–10 months per year — one of the highest rates in the country. That means your HVAC system is a major driver of your electricity bill. The good news: a few smart habits and one or two low-cost upgrades can meaningfully reduce what you pay, without sacrificing comfort.

Here are seven energy-efficiency tips our technicians recommend most to homeowners and businesses across the Tampa Bay area.

The 7 Tips

1

Upgrade to a Programmable or Smart Thermostat

A smart thermostat automatically adjusts temperatures based on your schedule — cooling the house before you arrive home and backing off while you're away or asleep. According to the U.S. Department of Energy, you can save up to 10% per year on heating and cooling by turning your thermostat back 7–10°F for eight hours a day.

Potential savings: up to 10% annually
2

Change Your Air Filter Regularly

A clogged air filter forces your system to work harder to pull air through, wasting energy and stressing the blower motor. In Tampa Bay's dusty, humid climate, we recommend checking your filter monthly and replacing it every 1–3 months. A clean filter can improve airflow efficiency by 5–15%.

Potential savings: 5–15% on airflow costs
3

Seal Duct Leaks

The U.S. EPA estimates that the average home loses 20–30% of conditioned air through duct leaks — gaps, holes, and poorly connected sections in your ductwork. That's cool air disappearing into your attic or walls instead of reaching your rooms. A professional duct inspection and sealing can recapture that lost efficiency significantly.

Potential savings: 20–30% of AC output recovered
4

Improve Insulation and Seal Air Leaks

In South Florida, the battle is keeping hot, humid air out — not cold air in. Check weatherstripping around doors and windows, and add caulking around gaps where pipes or wires enter the home. Better attic insulation is one of the highest-ROI improvements you can make in a Tampa Bay home, directly reducing how hard your AC has to work.

5

Use Ceiling Fans to Extend Your AC's Range

Ceiling fans don't lower the temperature — they create a wind-chill effect that makes you feel cooler. Running a fan lets you set your thermostat 4°F higher without any reduction in comfort, saving roughly 4% on cooling costs for each degree increase. Make sure fans rotate counterclockwise in summer to push cool air downward.

Potential savings: ~4% per degree thermostat increase
6

Keep the Outdoor Unit Clean and Clear

Your AC's outdoor condenser needs clear airflow to release heat efficiently. Keep the area around the unit free of overgrown shrubs, debris, and clutter — leave at least two feet of clearance on all sides. Rinse the condenser coils annually with a garden hose to remove dust and buildup. A dirty condenser can increase your energy consumption by 10–15%.

Potential savings: 10–15% on condenser efficiency
7

Schedule Annual Professional Maintenance

A properly maintained system runs at peak efficiency. During a tune-up, a technician checks refrigerant levels, cleans coils, tests electrical components, lubricates moving parts, and verifies that everything is running correctly. Systems that receive annual maintenance run 15–20% more efficiently than neglected systems — and they last years longer.

Potential savings: 15–20% system efficiency gain

Quick Wins That Cost Nothing

Beyond the tips above, these habits cost zero dollars and can reduce your cooling load immediately:

Close blinds and curtains on west-facing windows during afternoon hours
Run the dishwasher, dryer, and oven in the evening when it's cooler
Close off unused rooms and redirect airflow to occupied spaces
Set your thermostat to 78°F when home, 85°F when away

When It's Time for a System Upgrade

If your AC is more than 12–15 years old, no amount of maintenance will overcome its age-related inefficiency. Modern systems with high SEER ratings (Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio) can be 30–50% more efficient than units from the early 2000s. If your energy bills keep climbing despite regular maintenance, a system evaluation may reveal that replacement pays for itself within a few years in energy savings alone.
